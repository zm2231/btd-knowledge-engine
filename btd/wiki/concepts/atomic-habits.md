# Atomic Habits

James Clear's framework for behavior change through small, compounding improvements. The core idea: you don't rise to the level of your goals, you fall to the level of your systems.

## The Four Laws

1. **Make it obvious** — design your environment so the cue is visible. Want to read more? Put the book on your pillow. Want to code daily? Leave the editor open.
2. **Make it attractive** — pair the habit with something you enjoy. Study in a coffee shop you like. Pair boring exercises with a podcast.
3. **Make it easy** — reduce friction. The two-minute rule: scale any habit down to something that takes less than two minutes. "Read 30 pages" becomes "open the book."
4. **Make it satisfying** — immediate reward after the behavior. Track your streak. The visual progress itself becomes rewarding.

## Identity Over Outcomes

The most powerful idea for this system: focus on identity, not goals. "I want to lose weight" is a goal. "I'm the type of person who doesn't miss workouts" is an identity. Identity-based habits are stickier because you're not fighting yourself; you're becoming yourself.

This maps directly to experiment design. The experiment card doesn't say "consume 3 Karpathy videos." It says "become someone who builds things from scratch to understand them." The content is a means, not the end.

## The 1% Rule

1% better every day for a year = 37x improvement. 1% worse every day = nearly zero. The math of compounding applies to habits, not just money. This is why experiments are weekly: small enough to sustain, frequent enough to compound.

## When to Surface

- Any user designing their first experiment → the four laws structure the experiment
- `accountability_pattern` shows any failure pattern → diagnose which law is broken
- User is over-ambitious → the 1% rule and two-minute rule as calibration
- User has tried and failed before → identity-based reframe
