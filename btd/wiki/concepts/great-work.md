# Great Work

The question that cuts through everything: *what would you work on if you weren't trying to impress anyone?* Paul Graham's essay "How to Do Great Work" is a manual for choosing what to build and why — not how to build it faster, but how to know whether you're building the right thing in the first place. For BTD members who've already decided to ship with AI, this is the upstream question.

## Work on What You're Actually Curious About

The central premise is that great work requires genuine interest. Not "this seems like a good opportunity" or "this would be impressive" — actual curiosity that makes you want to know what happens next. The tell is whether you think about a problem when you don't have to. If you're only working on something when you're scheduled to, the interest is probably borrowed.

Graham distinguishes between **pretending to like** a field (common, often the result of status-seeking) and **actually liking** it (rarer, but immediately visible in the work). The former produces competent output. The latter produces work that surprises even the person doing it.

For BTD builders: the AI moment makes it easy to ship quickly in any direction. That's exactly when the question matters more, not less. You can now build almost anything — the constraint has shifted from "can I build it" to "is this what I actually want to exist."

## The Consistency Principle

Consistent shallow work compounds faster than occasional deep work. Graham's framing: work a little on something every day, even when you don't feel like it. The daily contact keeps the problem alive in your background process. Breakthroughs tend to happen not when you're staring at the problem but when you've been living with it long enough that a connection fires unexpectedly.

This cuts against the sprint-and-recover pattern most builders default to. The goal isn't heroic effort — it's maintaining the thread. Even 30 minutes counts, because the point is to not let the problem go cold.

## The "Impressive" Trap

One of the essay's most useful warnings: a lot of smart people end up working on things that *look* important rather than things that *are* important to them. The optimization is for how the work appears to peers, advisors, or the market — not for whether it pulls them forward.

The test Graham proposes: imagine you could work on anything, with no one watching and no one evaluating. What would you choose? The gap between that answer and what you're actually doing is diagnostic.

This is especially relevant for builders who've come up through contexts that reward prestige signals — certain kinds of startups look good on paper and feel hollow in practice. The work doesn't lie.

## Openness About Not Knowing

Graham argues that working on big problems requires tolerating ambiguity about whether you're doing the right thing. You won't know for a while. The research phase, the exploratory phase, the "I'm not sure this is going anywhere" phase — these are the work, not obstacles to the work.

The practical implication: be suspicious of certainty early. If your plan is fully locked down before you've built anything, you probably haven't thought about it hard enough. Great work tends to discover what it's actually about somewhere in the middle.

## For BTD Builders

The BTD community is for people who've already decided to ship. Great-work is the philosophy for what to ship and why.

The relevant questions to ask with a profile in hand:
- Is this user working on something they're genuinely curious about, or something that seems like a good idea?
- Is their experiment testing something they actually want to know, or something they feel like they should test?
- Are they stuck because of a skill gap — or because they're not sure the thing they're building is the right thing?

The second and third blockers look like the first from the outside. They both produce the same symptom: not shipping. But they have different solutions.

## When to Surface

- User is productive but feels like they're working on the wrong thing
- User is paralyzed before starting an experiment — might be a direction problem, not a skill problem
- User's stated goal sounds impressive but doesn't match their blind spots or natural curiosity
- Check-in reveals consistent low engagement despite technical progress
- User asks what they should build next with no constraints
