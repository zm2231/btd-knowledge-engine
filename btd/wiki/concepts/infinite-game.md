# The Infinite Game

From game theory: finite games have known players, fixed rules, and an agreed endpoint. Infinite games have known and unknown players, changeable rules, and no finish line. The objective is to keep playing, not to win.

## The Framework

**Finite game**: baseball. Known rules, agreed outcome, someone wins.
**Infinite game**: business, careers, learning. No endpoint. No "winning." Players drop out when they run out of will or resources.

The problem: most companies play business as a finite game. "We're trying to beat our competition." "We're number one." Based on what criteria? What timeframe? Who agreed to the rules?

## Why It Matters

Companies playing the finite game obsess over competition. Companies playing the infinite game obsess over their purpose. Sinek's example: at a Microsoft leadership summit (Ballmer era), 70% of executives spent 70% of time talking about beating Apple. At an Apple summit, 100% of executives spent 100% of time talking about helping teachers teach and students learn.

The infinite player frustrates the finite player. "I have no doubt" (Apple executive, when told Microsoft's Zune was better than iPod Touch). Conversation over. They're not competing against Microsoft; they're competing against themselves.

## Application

For builders: are you building to beat a competitor, or building toward something you believe in? The framing changes every decision. Jim Sinegal (Costco): "Public companies are looking to succeed for the quarter. We're looking for the next 50 years."

For learners: learning itself is an infinite game. There's no "I learned AI." There's only "I'm further along than yesterday." The intake interview's experiment model is fundamentally infinite-game thinking: time-boxed experiments, not endpoints.
