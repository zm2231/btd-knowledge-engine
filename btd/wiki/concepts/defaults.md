# Defaults

A default is the choice you make when you're not paying attention. It's the path of least resistance — the workflow you run on autopilot, the tool you reach for because it's what you've always used, the way you evaluate output because that's what "good" has meant for the last ten years. Defaults aren't failures. They're efficient. Until the world changes and your defaults don't.

That's what Beware the Defaults is named for. "Defaults" isn't a metaphor. It's the mechanism. Most people won't notice the ground has shifted until they're already behind. The community exists to surface the invisible assumptions that run underneath the way you work — and challenge them before the bill comes due.

## What a Default Looks Like

The bankrupt CEO in BTD's founding piece had decades of experience. When a bet-the-company decision arrived, he did what his training taught him: he matched the situation to a case study and acted. He was competent. He was confident. He picked the wrong file.

His default — "I've seen this before" — was so fast and invisible that it fired before he knew it had. He wasn't lazy. He wasn't arrogant. He was running a pattern that had served him for thirty years, in a situation where that pattern no longer applied.

That is the structure of every default failure: a behavior that was right in the environment it was built for, running unchanged in an environment that has since been redrawn.

## Three Layers of Default

BTD identifies three levels at which defaults operate, each harder to see than the last:

**Identity protection** — "I'm the expert. My value comes from knowing things." When AI output is good, this default gets uncomfortable. The defense is subtle: you don't reject the output, you just find reasons it's not quite right. You edit it back toward the version you would have written. It feels like maintaining standards. It's protecting an identity that's becoming less valuable.

**Perceptual filtering** — Your expertise filters your perception before you're aware anything's been blocked. You read AI output and see what your mental models expect to see, not what's actually there. BTD's example: a client who couldn't figure out why Claude kept "missing the point." She'd been using it for eight months. They asked her to read an output slowly, as if she'd never seen it before. She stopped halfway through: "That's actually interesting. I didn't see that the first three times." Her expertise had already decided what mattered. It feels like quality control.

**Possibility compression** — The quiet killer. When you approach anything with "I know what good looks like," you've already defined the target. Every prompt, every edit bends toward what you had in mind. You find what you expected to find. AI is most useful for finding the targets you didn't know existed — and this default eliminates them. It feels like knowing what you're doing.

## The AI Era Resets All Defaults

The reason BTD launched in January 2026 is that the default layer is being rewritten faster than most people can track. The frontier moves weekly. What you learned last month may already be obsolete. The capability that didn't exist yesterday might change everything.

The most dangerous version isn't the skeptic who refuses to use AI. It's the expert who uses it constantly and isn't getting better — and doesn't know why. They're plugging AI into their existing workflows like a faster version of what they already had. They're fitting a square peg into a round hole. And they're so focused on the fitting that they never stop to ask what a round peg might make possible.

The data BTD surfaces is striking: in a METR randomized control trial, experienced developers using AI tools took 19% longer to complete tasks than developers working without them. Those same developers believed AI had made them 24% faster. Wrong about direction. Wrong about magnitude. Wrong in both directions at once. That asymmetry is the default in action at scale.

## The Washing Machine Trap

The AI productivity story runs on the same assumption the washing machine did in the 1950s: do the same work faster, get the leftover time back. Ruth Schwartz Cowan studied what actually happened. By the 1970s, women were spending more time on laundry than before the machines arrived. The machines made laundry easier, so people washed more often. Standards quietly recalibrated upward. The time didn't come back — it got absorbed.

The same cycle is running for AI, compressed from decades into months. Workers finish tasks faster, take on more work, extend their hours because the tool is always available, and burn out under the weight of a bar that moved while nobody was watching. The capacity didn't create slack. It created demand.

This is a default operating at structural level. Nobody sends a memo. The new normal sets in because one person on the team figured out Claude first and reset the benchmark for everyone. You can't opt out of "clean" being redefined — you can only decide how to respond.

## The "Silent Killer" Frame

BTD's term for the default that feels like competence until it destroys you: the silent killer. It doesn't announce itself. It feels like judgment. Like standards. Like knowing what you're doing. Your ego will tell you this doesn't apply to you. That's how you know it does.

The I-35W bridge in Minneapolis collapsed in 2007. It had been inspected every single year. Every report was filed and signed. The NTSB found steel plates half the required thickness — a design error from 1967 that inspectors had been documenting for 40 years. The gap between "inspected" and "safe" grew one year at a time. The default was: follow the process. The process never included the step where someone says stop.

The parallel to AI review is direct. You generate a draft, glance at it, hit send. The review is happening — technically. But it's been compressed to the point where it creates the illusion of safety without the substance. "Looks right" quietly replaced "is right." Automation complacency, documented by Raja Parasuraman at George Mason: the more reliable the system, the worse humans become at catching its errors. The better the tool gets, the softer your guard becomes.

## Challenging Defaults

The BTD framework isn't digital detox or abstinence from AI. It isn't pessimism. The argument is the opposite: the opportunity available to people who can see and challenge their defaults is enormous.

The SEO analyst who was reviewing 8,000 keywords per client, 56 clients, once a week — an entire day of work every week — had built her entire workflow around that process. She had no reason to think it could change. Twenty minutes with someone who asked the right questions and built a Claude Project around her criteria: the analysis dropped to ten minutes. The look she had wasn't excitement. It was reorientation. Her mental model of what was possible quietly shifted and didn't shift back.

That's the flip side of defaults: most of the world is still running on yesterday's assumptions. The 90-million-dollar at-home care company in Missouri. The mid-sized law firm. The experienced developers at the Supabase hackathon who were slow not because they were bad but because their defaults were intact. Every one of those is a proof point available to anyone who can see past their own defaults long enough to show someone something new.

The BTD question — the one that disarms the default — is: **"What's here that I wouldn't have created?"** Not "is this what I wanted." Not "what needs fixing." Just: what's here that I didn't think of? Run that question for one week before evaluating AI output and you'll discover how thoroughly your expertise has been filtering out surprises.

## When to Surface

Surface this concept when a user:
- Is frustrated that AI tools aren't working as expected despite regular use
- Has deep domain expertise and is trying to figure out why their results feel flat
- Asks why their old workflows aren't translating to AI-assisted work
- Is consuming AI content (videos, articles, hot takes) but not building anything
- Expresses anxiety about being "behind" or "replaced"
- Needs framing for what BTD is and why it exists
