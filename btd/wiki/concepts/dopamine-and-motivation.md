# Dopamine and Motivation

Huberman's key insight: dopamine is about pursuit, not reward. The spike happens in anticipation of the reward, not when you get it. This one fact explains most motivation problems.

## The Baseline Problem

Your dopamine system has a baseline level. Activities that spike dopamine above baseline feel motivating. But here's the trap: after a spike, your baseline drops below where it started. The bigger the spike, the deeper the trough. This is why scrolling social media for an hour makes everything else feel boring afterward.

Stacking dopamine triggers makes it worse. Coffee + music + pre-workout + an exciting project = massive spike followed by massive trough. The recommendation: don't stack. Sometimes do hard things without any enhancement. Let the effort itself be the dopamine source.

## Why People Can't Sustain

The intake interview's `accountability_pattern` often reveals: "Starts strong, drops at week 3." Huberman's framework explains this. Week 1 is novel (dopamine spike from novelty). By week 3, the novelty is gone, baseline has dropped, and the same activity that felt exciting now feels like a grind.

The fix isn't motivation; it's understanding that the grind feeling IS the trough after the novelty spike. It passes. The people who sustain are the ones who know it's coming and push through it, not the ones with more willpower.

## Practical Protocols

- Cold exposure (cold shower/plunge) raises baseline dopamine for hours without a crash
- Intermittent reward schedules: don't celebrate every small win. Random reinforcement is more durable.
- Attach the reward to the effort, not the outcome. "I enjoy the process of learning" is more sustainable than "I'll feel great when I finish the course."

## When to Surface

- `accountability_pattern` shows motivation cycling (starts hot, fades)
- User asks why they can't stick with things → this explains the mechanism
- User is designing their first experiment → warn about the week 3 trough
