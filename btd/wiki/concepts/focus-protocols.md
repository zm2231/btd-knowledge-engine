# Focus Protocols

The neuroscience of attention — what focus actually is at the brain level, why it fails, and what specifically moves the needle. Huberman's frame is useful because it separates the mechanisms from the mythology. Most focus advice is behavioral (work in a quiet room, put your phone away). His goes one level deeper: what's happening in the brain, and what inputs actually change it.

## What Focus Is

Focus is not a state you either have or don't. It's a skill with a biological substrate. Two systems are involved:

1. **Attention narrowing** — the ability to lock onto something and exclude competing inputs. Controlled by the catecholamine system (dopamine/norepinephrine). ADHD is primarily a deficit here.
2. **Distraction suppression** — the separate ability to block out irrelevant information. Controlled by acetylcholine and visual focus (where your eyes point correlates with where your mind points).

These are distinct. Someone can have good narrowing but poor suppression, or vice versa. Most "I can't focus" problems are actually distraction-suppression problems — not a lack of attention, but an inability to hold it against the pull of other stimuli.

## The Protocol Stack

**Before the session:**
- **Visual focus drill**: stare at a fixed point for 30-60 seconds before starting work. This primes the attention-narrowing mechanism. It sounds too simple. It works.
- **Delay the first phone check**: the first 0-15 minutes of a focus session is the hardest; don't interrupt that window with any context shift.

**During the session:**
- **Expect the first 5-15 minutes to feel bad.** The resistance you feel at the start of a deep focus session is normal — it's the brain transitioning states, not evidence that you're doing it wrong. Most people quit before the transition completes.
- **Stress is not the enemy of focus; it's a prerequisite.** Low-level adrenaline (not panic) sharpens attention. The goal is not to eliminate that tension but to not confuse it with a signal to stop.

**After the session:**
- **Non-sleep deep rest (NSDR) / deliberate rest:** 10-20 minutes of eyes-closed, non-directed rest accelerates memory consolidation. This is when learning gets cemented. Skipping it is leaving gains on the table.

## The Training Model

Focus is trainable the same way physical endurance is: you build capacity by repeatedly going near your limit, recovering, and repeating. Current capacity is not fixed. But you can't sprint before you can walk — if someone can't focus for 10 minutes, jumping to 90-minute sessions won't work.

Start shorter than feels necessary. 25 minutes of genuine focus beats 90 minutes of distracted pretending.

## When to Surface for BTD Members

- Builder doing their first deep work session — give them the visual focus drill + "expect the bad first 15 minutes" framing
- Someone saying "I can't focus" — this reframes focus as trainable, not fixed
- Anyone whose `calibrated_level` is pre-beginner on any topic — learning efficiency depends on this
