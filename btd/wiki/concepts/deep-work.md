# Deep Work

Focusing without distraction on a cognitively demanding task. Cal Newport's definition is precise and narrow: it's not "hard work" or "important work." It's the specific activity of thinking hard about something hard without context-switching.

## Why It's Hard

Context shifts are the enemy. Every time you check email, glance at Slack, or respond to a notification, your brain takes 15-25 minutes to return to the same depth of focus. Most knowledge workers context-shift every 6 minutes. The math is brutal: you never get deep.

Newport's research: most people can sustain about 4 hours of deep work per day, max. Novices start at 1 hour. The goal isn't to fill your entire day with deep work; it's to protect those 1-4 hours and make them count.

## The System

1. **Time block.** Every minute of your day has a job. Deep work blocks get protected.
2. **Shutdown ritual.** At the end of the workday, review open tasks, capture loose ends, say "shutdown complete." Your brain stops ruminating.
3. **Lead measure.** Track hours of deep work per week, not output. Output is a lagging indicator; deep work hours are the leading indicator.
4. **Train.** Focus is a muscle. Reading books, productive meditation (thinking through a problem while walking), strategy games. If you haven't trained, you can't just "decide" to focus.

## Connection to Paul Graham

Graham's "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule" is the organizational version of this. Makers need 4-hour blocks. Managers schedule in 1-hour slots. If you're a maker trapped in a manager's schedule, deep work is impossible. The solution isn't willpower; it's restructuring your calendar.
