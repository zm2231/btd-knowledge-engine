# Build in Public

Building in private optimizes for comfort. Building in public optimizes for learning. The embarrassment of sharing unfinished work is not a bug — it's the tuition you pay for faster feedback and better judgment.

## The Core Idea

Most builders default to finishing before showing. Ship when it's polished. Share when it's impressive. Demo when you know what's coming.

The problem: polish takes time, and the wrong thing can get very polished very fast. You spend weeks refining the wrong architecture, the wrong feature, the wrong mental model — because nobody told you it was wrong until you were already done.

Building in public breaks that loop. It forces you to articulate what you're doing before you've fully figured it out. The act of explaining creates the feedback. The feedback shapes the build. The build gets better faster.

From the BTD Substack *Building With the Garage Door Open*:

> "Polished demos teach polish. They teach you to admire someone else's system. Messy problem-solving teaches something different. It teaches you how to think when the first approach doesn't work."

This is the core distinction. The polished demo is a performance — rehearsed, de-risked, optimized to look impressive. The open build is something else: apprenticeship. You watch the practitioner get stuck. You see how they think when the script breaks. That's what actually develops judgment.

## What It Looks Like in Practice

Build in public is not a marketing strategy. It's not a content strategy. It's an operating mode.

Concretely, it looks like:

- **Sharing the problem before you have the solution.** "Here's what I'm trying to figure out, here's where I'm stuck" — not "here's the finished thing I built."
- **Showing the dead ends.** The approaches that didn't work are as instructive as the ones that did. Most tutorials skip them.
- **Inviting interruption.** The feedback that changes your build is rarely the feedback you asked for. It comes from someone who saw something you missed because you were too close to it.
- **Letting the uncertainty show.** AI expertise ages in weeks. Certainty about workflow is a liability right now. The willingness to figure it out in public is a durable skill.

The BTD Build Lab is the applied version: no scripts, no rehearsals, real problems from community members, practitioners working through them in real time. Things break. Approaches fail. Everyone learns more than if it had been prepared.

## Why BTD Members Do This

For builders in the BTD community, building in public serves two distinct functions:

**As a learning accelerator.** The moment you commit to showing something, you think more carefully about it. Explaining forces understanding. Sharing invites the correction you didn't know you needed.

**As an accountability mechanism.** Private experiments are easy to abandon. Public experiments create a soft obligation to follow through and report back. That obligation is a feature — it's the [[accountability-pattern]] in action.

There's also a compounding effect. When you build in public consistently, you develop a record of your own thinking over time. You can see where your assumptions were wrong, which ideas held up, what actually worked. That record is the raw material for developing your own [[taste]].

## The Resistance

Most people don't do this because sharing unfinished work is uncomfortable. The polished demo feels safer. Announcing a project before it works feels premature. Showing a failed approach feels embarrassing.

That discomfort is real. But it's not a reason to work in private — it's evidence that the public constraint is actually doing something. The discomfort means you're being held to a standard you can't hide from.

The alternative is building in private until it's ready, which means no feedback until after the decisions are already baked in. The defaults are comfortable and slow. [[defaults]]

## When to Surface

Surface this concept when a user:

- Is reluctant to share early work ("I'll share it when it's done")
- Is building alone without external pressure or feedback loops
- Keeps restarting experiments without completing them
- Is copying someone else's system instead of discovering their own use cases
- Wants accountability but hasn't built any public constraint into their workflow
