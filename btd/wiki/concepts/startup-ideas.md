# Startup Ideas

The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to notice problems — preferably ones you have yourself. Graham's framework is anti-ideation: the best ideas don't come from brainstorming sessions, they come from living at the edge of a field that's changing and noticing what's broken.

## The Core Test

Good startup ideas have three things in common: the founders want it themselves, they can build it, and few others realize it's worth doing. That third criterion is the important one. If an idea is obvious, it's either already built or there's a reason nobody's doing it.

## The Well vs. The Crater

Two shapes of demand:
- **Broad but shallow**: many people mildly interested (social network for pet owners). Sounds big. Actually zero users at launch.
- **Narrow and deep**: few people want it urgently. This is what you want.

Ask: who wants this *right now*? Who wants it so badly they'd use a crappy v1 from a two-person startup they've never heard of? If you can't answer that, the idea is probably bad.

Google is a crater: hundreds of millions, want it a lot. A startup can't start there. Microsoft started as a well (Basic for Altair owners — a few thousand people who absolutely needed it). Facebook started as a well (Harvard students). The well is the entry point; the expansion path comes later.

## The "Sitcom Startup" Warning

Ideas generated by brainstorming tend to be "sitcom ideas" — ideas a TV writer would invent for a character starting a startup. They sound plausible. They test fine with friends ("yeah, maybe I could see using that"). They get zero users. The problem is they're invented, not discovered.

The tell: when you run a sitcom idea by someone, they say *"I could see other people wanting that."* When you run a real idea by the right person, they say *"when can I have this?"*

## How to Get Real Ideas

1. **Live at the leading edge.** Be at the frontier of something changing fast. You don't need to be pushing it forward — being a power user counts. Zuckerberg built Facebook because he was a student who understood what students needed, not because he was a brilliant product visionary.
2. **Notice what's missing, broken, or kludged.** If you're doing something and thinking "this should be easier" or building a workaround, that's signal.
3. **Work on hard problems.** Ideas cluster around difficulty. The harder the problem you're working on, the more likely the solution becomes a company.

## For BTD Builders

Most BTD members are building with AI before most of the world has figured out AI. That's the exact position Graham describes as valuable: at the leading edge, noticing what's broken.

The question to ask: what are you doing repeatedly where you think "this should be easier, someone should build this"? That's almost always where the real ideas are. Not "what problem could I solve" but "what problem am I already bumping into?"
