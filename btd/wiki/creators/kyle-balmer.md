# Kyle Balmer

A generalist AI educator aimed at curious non-technical people who want practical orientation — not deep builds, but confident use. Balmer covers the widest range of AI topics in the corpus: model comparisons, hallucination mechanics, agent demos, new feature reactions, and beginner tutorials for tools like OpenAI Codex. His style is casual and opinion-forward, closer to "knowledgeable friend explaining the news" than structured course. Where Mark Kashef assumes you want to ship something and teaches you how, Balmer meets people earlier in the journey — at the stage of "what even is this and should I care?" His content spans short-form clips (under 5 minutes) and longer beginner tutorials (40+ minutes), with most corpus entries being the former.

## Key Contributions

### [[ai-for-non-technical]] — AI Literacy for Skeptics and Beginners
Balmer is the corpus's strongest voice on AI fundamentals for non-technical audiences. His hallucination explainer ("yup! ChatGPT makes stuff up! here's why.") gives a plain-language account of why LLMs confabulate — framing them as advanced autocomplete, not databases — and introduces practical countermeasures including prompt specificity and his RISEN prompting framework (Role, Instruction, Steps, End goal, Narrowing). No jargon, no prerequisites.

### [[ai-agents]] — Practical First Steps
"Not sure how to use ChatGPT agents?" demonstrates agent mode through a concrete low-stakes task (coupon-code finding), using it to illustrate the key principle: agents shine on multi-step repetitive tasks, not one-shot questions. A good entry point for BTD members who've heard "agentic AI" and don't know where to start.

### AI Coding Tools for Beginners — Codex Walkthrough
"Complete Beginner's Guide to OpenAI's Codex App" (43 min) is the corpus's most thorough Balmer tutorial — a step-by-step setup guide covering GitHub basics, repo setup, and the shift from traditional IDEs to AI-native coding environments. Honest about Codex's current limitations vs. easier alternatives like Lovable or Replit. Useful for BTD members at the pre-builder stage who want to understand the AI coding landscape before committing to a tool.

### [[prompt-design]] — RISEN Framework
The RISEN framework (Role, Instruction, Steps, End goal, Narrowing) appears across multiple Balmer pieces as his personal prompting scaffold. Practical and beginner-friendly — designed to reduce ambiguity in prompts without requiring prompt engineering expertise.

### AI Landscape Commentary
A cluster of short clips covering model comparisons (Grok, Claude, Gemini, ChatGPT), new feature reactions (Claude's file-editing capability, OpenAI announcements), and AI-in-society observations (academic cheating, political bias in models). Useful as quick orientation for BTD members who want to understand the competitive landscape without reading tech news.

## When to Surface This Creator

- User is **pre-beginner or early beginner** and needs foundational orientation → hallucination explainer, agents clip
- User asks **"why does AI make things up?"** or "can I trust AI outputs?" → hallucination video is the clearest answer in the corpus
- User wants to understand **what agents actually do** with a concrete example → agents coupon clip
- User is exploring **AI coding tools** but hasn't committed to one yet → Codex beginner guide, with the honest caveat that Lovable/Replit may still be easier starting points
- User wants a **quick mental model** of how different AI models compare → landscape commentary clips
- `calibrated_level` is `pre-beginner` or `curious-non-technical` → lead with Balmer before Kashef
- User has already engaged with Kashef's deep dives and wants **lighter follow-up content** between experiments
