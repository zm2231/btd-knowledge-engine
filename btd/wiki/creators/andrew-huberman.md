# Andrew Huberman

Stanford neuroscience professor. His content gives the biological layer underneath everything else in the corpus. Where Newport tells you to time-block and Abdaal tells you to study with active recall, Huberman explains the dopamine and neuroplasticity mechanisms that make those techniques work (or fail).

## Key Contributions

### [[dopamine-and-motivation]]
"Controlling Your Dopamine For Motivation, Focus & Satisfaction" (12M views) is the cornerstone. Dopamine isn't about reward; it's about pursuit. The spike happens in anticipation, not achievement. Practical upshot: if you layer too many dopamine triggers (phone + music + coffee + pre-workout), you raise your baseline and everything feels less motivating. This directly explains why some users' intake profiles show "starts strong, can't sustain."

### [[focus-protocols]]
"How to Focus to Change Your Brain" and "ADHD & How Anyone Can Improve Their Focus" cover the neuroplasticity of attention. Focus is a skill that improves with practice, not a personality trait. 90-minute focus blocks match ultradian cycles. Visual focus (literally narrowing your gaze) triggers the neurochemistry of cognitive focus.

### [[evidence-based-learning]]
"Optimal Protocols for Studying & Learning" is the neuroscience complement to Abdaal's practical study tips. Spacing, testing, and interleaving work because of how memory consolidation happens during sleep. Sleep is not optional for learning; it's where the learning actually gets encoded.

### Habits
"The Science of Making & Breaking Habits" connects to James Clear's work but from the neuroscience angle. Habit formation is dopamine-dependent; the key is linking the reward to the effort, not the outcome.
