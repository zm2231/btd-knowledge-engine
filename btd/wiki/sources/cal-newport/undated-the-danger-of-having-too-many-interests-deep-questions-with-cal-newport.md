# The Danger Of Having Too Many Interests | Deep Questions With Cal Newport

Newport answers a listener who quit their job to pursue multiple interests simultaneously and is making no meaningful progress on any of them. The core argument: effort-reward is nonlinear. Splitting time across ten projects doesn't produce ten times the value of one project — it produces far less than concentrating on one. The solution is to baseline everything except one primary focus, then rotate primary focus as milestones are hit.

## Key Ideas
- Effort-to-reward is nonlinear: concentrated focus on one thing produces discontinuous jumps in outcomes that split attention never reaches
- "Baselining" means maintaining something with minimal ongoing effort (a habit or ritual) while investing primary time elsewhere
- At any given time, only one or two things should receive serious attention — everything else is on baseline maintenance
- The rotating-focus model: go deep on one thing until a reward discontinuity hits, then redirect energy to the next priority
- Having too many interests isn't a personality flaw — it just requires a sequencing system to avoid chronic dilution
