# How to study for exams - Evidence-based revision tips

Drawing on Professor Dunlosky's landmark review of ten study techniques, Ali makes the case that the three most popular methods — rereading, highlighting, and summarizing — are rated "low utility" by the evidence, while active recall (practice testing) is rated "high utility." He explains the mechanism: retrieving information from memory strengthens neural connections; passively re-reading does not. Three practical implementations: Anki flashcards, drawing spider diagrams with the book closed, and writing questions for yourself (Cornell method) that you later answer from memory.

## Key Ideas
- Rereading, highlighting, and note-taking feel productive but have low evidence of retention benefit
- Active recall: the act of retrieving information IS the learning, not just a test of it
- Practice testing outperforms re-reading four times on both factual and inference questions (2011 study)
- Students systematically misjudge which techniques work — intuition is backwards
- Anki flashcards + spaced repetition automate the spacing schedule so you don't have to think about it
