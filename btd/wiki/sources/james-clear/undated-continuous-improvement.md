# Continuous Improvement

Clear's guide to mastering kaizen-style improvement — the art of getting 1% better on a consistent basis. The article distinguishes between breakthrough moments (visible) and the accumulation of marginal gains (invisible but causal). He uses the British Cycling team as the primary case study: coach Dave Brailsford's strategy of finding 1% improvements in dozens of areas (bike seats, pillow firmness, hand-washing technique) produced five Tour de France wins in six years. The principle applies equally to building software, writing, or any skill with measurable dimensions.

## Key Ideas
- Marginal gains aggregate: 1% better in 50 small areas produces massive compound improvement
- Most breakthroughs are the visible tip of a long invisible accumulation of small changes
- Continuous improvement requires measuring the right things — you can only improve what you track
- The aggregation of marginal gains works in reverse too: 1% worse across many factors compounds into failure
- Improvement is a system, not a single decision — it requires habits and environment design, not willpower
