# Identity-Based Habits

Clear's argument that the most durable form of behavior change starts with identity, not outcomes. Most people work outside-in: they set an outcome goal, design a process to achieve it, and hope the identity follows. Clear argues for inside-out: decide who you want to be, then prove it to yourself through small actions. Each habit vote — even a tiny one — casts a vote for your identity. Over time the accumulated votes reshape your self-image, and behavior that aligns with identity requires no willpower. "I am a writer" sustains writing through resistance; "I want to write more" doesn't.

## Key Ideas
- Two layers of change: outcome (what you want), process (what you do), identity (who you are) — identity is the deepest
- Identity-based habits: decide who you want to be, then take small actions that prove it to yourself
- Every action is a vote for or against your desired identity — small wins compound into a new self-image
- "I can't" vs. "I don't": identity language changes what feels possible vs. what feels like deprivation
- True behavior change is identity change — until you believe you're the type of person who does X, you'll always feel like you're forcing it
