# The Habit Loop

Clear's explanation of the neurological habit loop — cue, routine, reward — originally described by Charles Duhigg and extended in *Atomic Habits*. The article explains why habits persist even when consciously unwanted (the brain automates routines triggered by cues), how to interrupt the loop (change the routine while keeping the cue and reward), and how environment design can make cues for good habits more prominent while hiding cues for bad ones. The craving — the anticipatory desire for the reward — is the engine of the loop, not the reward itself.

## Key Ideas
- Habit loop: cue triggers craving, craving motivates routine, routine delivers reward, reward reinforces the cue
- The craving (anticipation) drives behavior, not the reward itself — dopamine spikes on cue, not on reward receipt
- Habits automate to save cognitive resources; this is why they're hard to break — the loop runs without conscious thought
- To change a habit: keep the cue and reward, swap the routine (the "Golden Rule of Habit Change")
- Environment is the most powerful cue system — what surrounds you determines what habits run automatically
