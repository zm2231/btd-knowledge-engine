# How to Do Great Work

Graham's most comprehensive attempt to distill the patterns of great work across fields into a single essay. The recipe: find work you have natural aptitude for and deep interest in, learn enough to reach the frontier, notice gaps others overlook, and pursue them boldly. But execution requires a specific kind of excited curiosity — both the engine and the rudder. He covers what to do when you don't know what to work on (work on things anyway; the direction becomes clear through doing), how to maintain morale, the role of colleagues, and why the most dangerous creative move is to imitate prestigious forms rather than following genuine interest.

## Key Ideas
- Four steps: choose a field, learn to the frontier, notice gaps, explore promising ones — this is how great work actually gets done
- "Excited curiosity" drives harder than mere diligence and also shows you what to work on
- Boldly chase outlier ideas that others ignore, especially if you have enough expertise to say precisely what they're overlooking
- Prolificness matters: the way to make great things is to make many things; most great work comes from people who produced a lot
- The most dangerous mistake is being ambitious about the wrong dimension — pursuing prestige over genuine interest
