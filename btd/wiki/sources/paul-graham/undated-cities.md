# Cities and Ambition

Graham argues that great cities send a message — a single dominant type of ambition that shapes everyone who lives there. New York says: make more money. Cambridge says: be smarter. Silicon Valley says: have more effect on the world. These messages are ambient and hard to resist; even someone with Leonardo's natural ability couldn't beat the force of environment. For builders, the implication is direct: surround yourself with people who care about the same things you do, because encouragement and discouragement are asymmetric — it's easier to kill ambition than to build it.

## Key Ideas
- Cities don't just attract ambitious people — they shape what kind of ambition you develop by what they admire most
- Most ambitious people assume they can transcend their environment; historical evidence suggests otherwise
- The critical years are the early and middle career, when you need peers and encouragement to push forward
- Admiration is zero-sum: a city focused on one type of ambition depresses all others (professors are second-class citizens in NY until they start hedge funds)
- You can leave a city once you've found your peers and your direction — the Impressionists all ended up scattered, but Paris defined them
