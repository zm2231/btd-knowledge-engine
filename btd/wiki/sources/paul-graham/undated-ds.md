# Do Things that Don't Scale

One of Graham's most practically actionable essays. Startups don't take off on their own — founders have to push them. The most important unscalable thing early on is recruiting users manually and delighting them obsessively. Stripe's founders installed merchants on the spot rather than sending a link. Airbnb went door-to-door in New York. The error is thinking these tactics are beneath a real company; in fact, the feedback you get from the first handful of users is the highest-quality feedback you'll ever get, and the habits you build (aggressive acquisition, obsessive service) become company DNA.

## Key Ideas
- Startups need a "crank" to get going — the engine won't keep running without a separate, laborious process to start it
- "Collison installation" principle: don't ask if they want to try your product, set them up on the spot
- The early unscalable work is not a necessary evil — it's where you learn things you could never learn from scale
- Contained fire strategy: start with a deliberately narrow market to get critical mass before expanding
- The question to ask about early-stage startups: "how big could this get if founders did the right things?" not "is this taking over the world?"
