# The Shape of AI: Jaggedness, Bottlenecks and Salients

A rigorous framework for understanding why AI progress feels simultaneous and uneven. Mollick revisits the "jagged frontier" concept and introduces two additional concepts: bottlenecks (single weak points that prevent a whole workflow from automating, even when the AI is mostly capable) and reverse salients (borrowing historian Thomas Hughes's term for the one lagging factor holding back an entire system). When a reverse salient breaks — as image quality did for presentations — capabilities that were blocked for years come flooding through at once.

## Key Ideas
- The jagged frontier means AI can be superhuman at diagnosis and still fail at running a vending machine — the shape doesn't map to human intuitions about difficulty
- Bottlenecks often aren't about AI ability at all: clinical trials still need human patients; FDA review still runs at institution speed
- Reverse salients create "lurches" — sudden jumps in capability when a single lagging factor gets fixed
- Memory and real-time learning are current reverse salients; when they break, the downstream capability release will be large
- Humans remain useful where bottlenecks exist — the jagged frontier protects many jobs even as others disappear
