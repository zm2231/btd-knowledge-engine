# Hold Attention, Not Devices

A brief but pointed demonstration: Sinek borrows a phone and holds it while talking — not checking it, just holding it — and asks the room whether they feel like the most important thing to him. They don't. The physical presence of a device signals divided attention even when no one is actively using it. For BTD builders, this is a crisp illustration of how defaults (phone always present) shape interpersonal dynamics in ways that undermine trust and felt importance.

## Key Ideas
- The mere presence of a phone on a table or in your hand signals that the person you're with is not your top priority
- Attention is what people actually feel — holding a device while "not checking it" still communicates divided loyalty
- This is a defaults problem: the phone is always out by default, so undivided attention requires a deliberate override
- Real estate agents, salespeople, and leaders walking with clients lose connection without realizing it through this habit
- Putting the device away entirely — not face-down, entirely — is the only reliable signal of full presence
