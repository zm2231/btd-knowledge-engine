# Sometimes, You Don't Have to Prove Yourself

Sinek makes the point that when someone gives you a shot — a job, a stage, an introduction — you've already been vetted. The proving is done. The anxious over-performing that follows (speaking in every meeting, asserting credentials constantly) misreads the situation. He uses Chanel's rule — no new senior executive speaks in meetings for the first 90 days — as an institutional answer to this instinct: listen, observe, understand before contributing.

## Key Ideas
- Being given a position or opportunity is itself evidence you've already been vetted — the audition is over
- New hire over-performance (proving yourself constantly) signals insecurity, not competence, to experienced observers
- Chanel's 90-day silence rule is a structural intervention against this default — it forces listening over asserting
- The urge to prove yourself comes from borrowed judgment: evaluating your worth through others' imagined assessment
- Confident presence often looks like listening intently and speaking selectively — not like filling every silence
