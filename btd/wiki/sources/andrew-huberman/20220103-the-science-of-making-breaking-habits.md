# The Science of Making & Breaking Habits

A neuroscience-grounded breakdown of habit formation and disruption. Huberman covers the limbic friction model — how habits compete with conscious effort — and introduces the concept of a "habit line" where behaviors become automatic. Discusses why context and time-of-day matter for habit installation, and why the first few days of a new habit feel hardest. Practical protocols include habit stacking, reward timing, and the 21/90 day model for consolidation.

## Key Ideas
- Habits form when neurological pathways lower the friction required to execute a behavior automatically
- Limbic friction is highest early in habit formation — expect the first two weeks to feel effortful by design
- Context cues (location, time, preceding actions) are as important as willpower for triggering habits
- Breaking a habit requires substitution, not just suppression — the neural groove needs a replacement
- Interleaving reward with effort (not just after) accelerates habit consolidation via dopamine timing
