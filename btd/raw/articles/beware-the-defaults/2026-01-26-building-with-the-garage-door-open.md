> “Most people avoid ‘magic show’ demos because they can’t predict what will get thrown at them.”

That’s what Charles wrote after Friday’s Build Lab. He wasn’t being critical. He was naming the thing most AI educators won’t admit.

The polished demo is a performance. The presenter knows what’s coming because they rehearsed it. Three times. Maybe five. Until the rough edges disappeared and everything worked on the first try.

It looks impressive. It feels professional.

**It teaches you almost nothing about what happens when things don’t work.**

* * *

There’s a growing industry built on a simple promise: watch experts, copy their systems.

It sounds reasonable. Find someone who’s figured it out. Learn their workflow. Apply it to your work.

Here’s the problem nobody talks about.

Every person has unique AI use cases. Your context. Your problems. Your way of thinking. Your messy combination of skills and gaps and weird edge cases that no guru anticipated when they built their “ultimate workflow.”

No expert’s system is your system. The best they can do is show you what’s possible. **The discovery is yours to make.**

But discovery is messy. It doesn’t fit in a 90-minute session with a clean arc and a satisfying conclusion. It involves getting stuck. Trying things that don’t work. Backtracking. Questioning whether you’re even asking the right question.

That’s not content you can rehearse.

* * *

Here’s what we’ve been experimenting with.

Every Friday, we turn on the camera. Community members bring problems. Real ones. Problems they’re actually stuck on, not softballs designed to make us look good.

We don’t know what’s coming.

Charles called it “live demo improvisation, tag-team problem solving, and real-time troubleshooting without flinching.”

That last part is generous. We flinch. We get stuck. We try approaches that go nowhere. We backtrack.

But we do it in the open.

* * *

Most AI education follows a predictable structure.

Expert presents system. System looks clean. Audience copies system. Audience wonders why their version doesn’t work as well.

The gap isn’t skill. It’s context.

The expert built that system for their problems. Their data. Their workflow. Their weird combination of needs that emerged over months of iteration.

When you copy it, you’re copying the output without the journey. You get the what without the why. The answer without understanding why that answer worked for that specific problem.

Polished demos teach polish. They teach you to admire someone else’s system.

Messy problem-solving teaches something different. It teaches you how to think when the first approach doesn’t work. How to notice what’s actually happening instead of what you expected to happen. How to improvise when the script breaks.

**That’s the skill that compounds.** Not “here’s my system.” But “here’s how I navigate when I don’t have a system yet.”

* * *

We’re not claiming to have this figured out.

Three weeks ago, Beware the Defaults was an idea and a group chat. Now we have a community, a handful of Build Labs under our belt, and people like Charles telling us something is working.

Small sample size. Early days.

But the signal is clear enough to keep going.

What we’re building isn’t another place to watch experts perform expertise. It’s a place where practitioners figure things out together. Where the value isn’t in having answers but in showing how you find them.

Four people with different skills. We all think about AI through different lenses: automations, development, use case discovery, workflows. Our approaches are different. Our backgrounds are different. But we’re aligned on the same thing: curiosity, challenging AI’s first response, discovering what’s actually possible instead of accepting what’s handed to us.

We cover ground together because we see different things.

We help you discover your use cases. We don’t hand you ours.

* * *

Halfway through one of these sessions, it hit us: this isn’t content. This is apprenticeship.

The old model. Before “content” became a product.

You don’t watch the master give lectures. You watch them get stuck. You see them solve problems they haven’t solved before. You learn how they think by being present while they think.

That’s harder to scale. You can’t pre-record it and sell access to the replay library.

But it’s how people actually develop judgment. Not technique. **Judgment.** The thing that tells you which technique to use when the situation doesn’t match the tutorial.

AI expertise ages in weeks. The guru who learned six months ago is already behind. The workflow that worked in October might be obsolete by February.

What doesn’t age: curiosity, experimentation, the willingness to get stuck in public and figure it out anyway.

* * *

This is what Fridays look like at Beware the Default.

No scripts. No rehearsals. Someone brings a problem. We solve it together. Things break. Things work. Everyone learns more than if we’d prepared.

If that sounds like too much uncertainty, there are plenty of places that offer polished demos and expert systems. That’s not what we’re building.

We’re building something different.

Bring a problem. Watch us figure it out. Figure out your own stuff alongside us.

The people who learn fastest aren’t the ones with the best tutorials. They’re the ones willing to look stupid together.
