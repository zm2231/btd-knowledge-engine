# The Silent Killer of the AI Era

**Creator**: beware-the-defaults  
**Date**: 2026-01-01T21:30:23.000Z  
**URL**: https://bewarethedefault.substack.com/p/the-silent-killer-of-the-ai-era  
**Platform**: Substack

The economist Russ Roberts once recounted a private conversation with a former CEO. The man’s company had gone bankrupt. The kind with a lobby and a board and his name in the Wall Street Journal.

He’d just lost his job and Roberts wasn’t about to bring it up so he was surprised when the CEO said:

_“You know, when I had to make that key decision, I used the wrong case study.”_

He was a Harvard MBA. Years of training in exactly this: match the situation to the case, then act.

So, when the moment came, when everything hung on one “bet-the-company” decision, he did what his training taught him to do. He shuffled through his mental files:

- The ones from business school.

- The ones from case competitions in conference rooms with bad coffee.

- The ones from the last three recessions.


He scanned the library of historical cases he’d built over decades and found the one that seemed to match his situation.

And he picked it...

But he picked wrong.

He had _too much experience_. That was the problem. His expertise gave him a library and his confidence made him certain he’d pulled the right file.

**The combination destroyed his company.**

## The Version That Looks Like Competence

We talk about ego like it’s arrogance. Chest-puffing. Credit-taking. The loud guy in the meeting who makes everything about himself.

That version is easy to spot.

But there’s another kind. The kind that feels like judgment. Like standards. Like knowing what you’re doing.

The bankrupt CEO wasn’t arrogant. He was competent. He’d built a mental library across a career. When the crisis hit, he reached for it. He had the knowledge. That was never the problem.

**The problem was trusting his pattern-matching more than the possibility that this situation might be different.**

His expertise became a filter. He saw what matched his existing models and missed what didn’t.

## **Your Expertise Doesn’t Want You To See This**

Your expertise has been protecting you for years. It sits in the background of every meeting, every decision, every quick judgment you make without thinking. It whispers, “You’ve seen this before. You know what this is.”

Usually, it’s right. That’s why you trust it.

But expertise has a problem. It gets good, then it gets invisible, then it gets defensive. It starts gatekeeping your inputs before you’re aware anything’s been blocked. It assumes the new thing is just a variation of the old thing. It does not raise its hand and say, “I might be wrong about this.”

This is what’s happening right now with AI.

Not to the skeptics. The skeptics aren’t the problem. They’ll come around or they won’t.

**The problem is the experts.**

The high-agency professionals who use AI every day and have strong opinions about how work should get done. The ones who take their existing workflows and try to plug AI into them like a faster version of what they already had.

They’re not failing to adopt the technology. They’re using it constantly. They’re just not getting better...

**And they don’t know why.**

We didn’t know why either. For the first year, we kept trying to make AI fit our old ways of working. Same workflows, same outputs, same assumptions about what “good” looked like.

We were fitting a square peg into a round hole.

And were so focused on the fitting that we never stopped to ask **what a round peg might make possible.**

## **Three Ways Your Brain Lies To You**

Here is what we’ve started to notice. Ego operates on three levels, and each one is harder to see than the last.

### **Level 1: Identity Protection**

This is the obvious one. “I’m the smart one.” “I’m the expert.” “My value comes from knowing things other people don’t.”

When AI produces something good, this version of ego gets uncomfortable. If the machine can do what you do, what does that say about you?

The defense mechanism is subtle. You don’t reject the output. You just find reasons why it’s not quite right. You edit it back toward the version you would have written. You maintain the feeling that you’re still the one who really knows.

- **The Cost**: Slower adaptation. You’re protecting an identity that’s becoming less valuable by the hour.

- **Feels Like:** Maintaining standards.


### **Level 2: Perceptual Filtering**

This one is trickier. Your expertise gives you strong pattern recognition. You’ve seen hundreds of situations like this. You know what good looks like.

The problem is your pattern recognition now filters your perception before you’re aware of it. When you read AI output, you’re not seeing what’s actually there. You’re seeing what your mental models expect to see.

A client once told us she couldn’t figure out why Claude kept “missing the point” of her prompts. She’d been using AI for eight months. She considered herself advanced. We asked her to read one of the outputs aloud, slowly, as if she’d never seen it before.

She stopped halfway through.

“That’s actually interesting,” she said. “I didn’t see that the first three times.”

She wasn’t lying. She literally hadn’t seen it. Her expertise had already decided what mattered and what didn’t.

- **The Cost**: Missed information. You’re not seeing what’s actually there.

- **Feels Like**: Quality control.


### **Level 3: Possibility Compression**

This is the quiet killer.

When you approach AI with “I know what good looks like,” you’ve already defined the target. The entire interaction becomes about hitting that target. Every prompt, every edit, every judgment bends the output toward what you already had in mind.

You already know how to hit the targets you’ve defined, BUT, AI is useful for finding the ones you didn’t know existed.

The Zen Buddhists have a phrase:

_“In the beginner’s mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert’s mind there are few.”_

They were talking about enlightenment. They were also talking about working with AI in 2026.

They just didn’t know it yet.

The expert who “knows what good looks like” is compressing the possibility space down to known territory. The beginner who doesn’t know is free to see what’s actually there.

- **The Cost**: Missed discovery. You’re finding what you expected to find.

- **Feels Like**: Knowing what you’re doing.


## **Why This Worked Until It Didn’t**

Here’s what ties these three levels together.

In a stable environment, expertise is an asset. You’ve seen this before. You know what works. Your accumulated mental models are correct more often than they’re wrong. Trusting your judgment is the right move.

In this unstable environment, expertise becomes a liability. The ground is shifting. Your mental models were built for a world that no longer exists. Trusting your judgment means trusting a map of a territory that’s been redrawn.

AI has made the environment unstable.

- The frontier moves weekly

- The thing you learned last month may already be obsolete.

- The capability that didn’t exist yesterday might change everything.


Your expertise doesn’t know this. It still thinks it’s 2023... and it still thinks it knows what good looks like.

Every hour you spend defending your priors, gatekeeping your inputs, and squeezing possibilities down to the familiar is an hour someone else spent wandering into territory you’ll never see.

## **This Isn’t Just You**

The same pattern plays out at the organizational level.

Large companies with massive AI budgets are getting outrun by three-person startups. Not because the startups are smarter but because the enterprise has too much to protect.

Existing workflows. Existing processes. Existing definitions of what “good” looks like. The AI implementation becomes a project to make the new thing fit the old thing. Every integration decision filters through “how do we preserve what we have?”

The startup has nothing to preserve so they ask a different question:

_**“What’s possible now?”**_

Same mechanism with a **completely** different scale.

## **The One Question That Disarms It**

The fix is not humility in the soft sense. Not self-deprecation or pretending you don’t know things.

The fix is a different question. Let’s make it concrete.

Before you evaluate AI output, before you decide what’s wrong with it, before you start editing it back toward the document you already had in your head, ask yourself:

**“What’s here that I wouldn’t have created?”**

- Not: “Is this what I wanted?”

- Not: “What needs to be fixed?”

- Not: “How do I make this sound like me?”


Just: “What’s here that I wouldn’t have thought of?”

This one question disarms all three traps. You’re not asking if AI is better than you. You’re not looking for the familiar. You’re not evaluating.

**You’re exploring.**

Try it for one week. Before you judge any AI output, find one thing that surprises you. Write it down in a note on your phone, or a sticky note next to your monitor, or the margin of whatever you’re working on. (Yes, even if you’re certain the output is wrong. Especially then.) Don’t evaluate whether the surprise is good or bad. **Just notice it.**

You will discover that you’ve been filtering out surprises since March. Your expertise has been doing its job so well that you stopped seeing what was actually there.

## **Your Ego Is Reading This Right Now**

Your ego will tell you this doesn’t apply to you.

That’s how you know it does.

- It will feel like standards.

- It will feel like quality control.

- It will feel like knowing what you’re doing.

- It will feel like all the things that made you successful in the first place.

- It will feel like your third cup of coffee telling you that you’re the only one who really understands the assignment.


**The question is whether you can see past it.**

Beginner’s mind doesn’t end when you become an expert. It’s a discipline you maintain against the gravitational pull of your own success. The Japanese calligraphers practice the same strokes for decades. The Zen monks meditate for years. Steve Jobs talked about staying foolish.

[Steve Jobs: Stay Hungry, Stay Foolish](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8Zx04h24uBs)

_(one of our favorite speeches of all time)_

Not because ignorance is good. Because openness requires continuous effort. Ego is gravity. It pulls you toward closure, toward certainty, toward the known.

The AI era rewards the people who can resist that pull.

- **High Agency + High Ego = Failure.**

- **High Agency + High Humility = Discovery.**


The choice is yours.

And if you’re reading this on January 1st, you already know why we picked today to say it.
