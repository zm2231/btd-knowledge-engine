# The Death of How-To Content

**Creator**: beware-the-defaults  
**Date**: 2026-01-11T13:02:36.000Z  
**URL**: https://bewarethedefault.substack.com/p/the-death-of-how-to-content

There used to be a job called encyclopedia salesman.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!h81D!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9e86a014-8333-4a18-b0e0-441fbb4953ad_338x341.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!h81D!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9e86a014-8333-4a18-b0e0-441fbb4953ad_338x341.png)

Door to door. Thirty-two pounds of books in a leather briefcase. The pitch landed in living rooms across America…

Your children need access to information. These fancy books are how they get it.

Then libraries got better.

Then the internet happened.

The “how do I find information about X” problem got solved for free.

The encyclopedia salesman didn’t pivot. He didn’t rebrand. He vanished. Along with his briefcase, his commission structure, and his optimism about the letter Q.

The same thing is happening to expert content right now. Three shifts. All at once.

* * *

## Shift 1: Access

“How do I write a cold email.” “How do I structure a presentation.” “How do I analyze this data.”

These questions have answers now. Good answers. Free answers. Answers that show up in a chat box before you finish typing the question.

The person who built an audience on “here’s how to do the thing you already know you want to do” is standing on a shrinking island.

The water rises every time someone opens Claude instead of clicking their tutorial. Every time someone pastes a transcript and gets a summary in four seconds instead of watching a 47-minute YouTube video about summarization frameworks.

How-to content isn’t worthless…yet. But the floor is dropping fast.

**And the elevator doesn’t go back up.**

* * *

## Shift 2: Value

In 1963, Julia Child dropped a potato pancake on the stove.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ilb1!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdb7eb08e-7cc4-49c1-8ef3-369e1682bfad_1640x1186.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ilb1!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdb7eb08e-7cc4-49c1-8ef3-369e1682bfad_1640x1186.png)

She was filming _The French Chef_ on live television. No second takes. The pancake slipped off the pan, hit the burner, and started to come apart in her hands.

She scooped it back up with her bare hands. Hot pan….on camera.

“When you flip anything, you just have to have the courage of your convictions,” she said. Then she kept cooking.

Before Julia Child, cooking instruction meant recipes.

- Ingredients

- Temperatures

- Steps


“How to make beef bourguignon” was a list in a book. Anyone could print it. Anyone could photocopy it and stick it in a drawer next to seventeen other recipes they’d never make.

She made it a demonstration.

You weren’t learning how to flip a pancake.

**You were learning what was** _**possible**_ when you stopped being afraid of the pan.

The dropped pancake taught more than the perfect flip ever could.

When “how” becomes free, value shifts to “what’s possible that you didn’t know to ask about.”

* * *

## Shift 3: Position

There’s a defensive benefit to this shift.

When you teach “how to,” you invite argument. Someone always knows a better way.

The comments fill with “actually, you should use a different framework” from a guy whose LinkedIn headline contains the word “evangelist.”

When you demonstrate “here’s how I’m doing it,” you’re showing your work. You can’t be wrong about your own process.

“I’m not saying this is the right way. I’m saying this is what I do. Decide for yourself if it’s useful.”

Different posture. Harder to attack. More interesting to watch.
