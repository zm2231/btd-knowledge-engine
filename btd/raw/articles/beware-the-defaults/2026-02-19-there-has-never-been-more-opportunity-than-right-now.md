# There Has Never Been More Opportunity Than Right Now

**Creator**: beware-the-defaults  
**Date**: 2026-02-19T13:30:59.000Z  
**URL**: https://bewarethedefault.substack.com/p/there-has-never-been-more-opportunity  
**Platform**: Substack

Last week, an article about AI got 80 million views.

The argument…

> The world as we know it is ending. Your job is next. Start preparing now.

So you can guess what happened next.

People shared it. Everywhere. Developers reported mental health crises on X.

A tech writer who covers this space for a living had to publish a counter-piece just to clear the air. Friends were texting friends. LinkedIn was a wall of anxiety.

I read the whole thing.

**Then I closed the tab and went back to work.**

### **What My Actual Week Looked Like**

I connected Claude directly to a client’s Webflow CMS, controlling the entire content management system, building wireframes, uploading assets.

I used Claude Code to build a voice agent designed to interview fifty sales reps, extract their pain points, and help build custom solutions for each of them.

Both of these things were done just by typing in plain english to AI.

But the moment that stuck with me most was MUCH simpler than either of those.

I helped an employee of a retainer client set up a Claude Project. Her job involves reviewing keywords for SEO campaigns. Not a few keywords. Somewhere between six and eight thousand of them. Per client. She has 56 clients.

The process of flagging the negative ones, keywords that were wasting ad spend, used to take her an entire day. Every week. For every client.

I want to be clear about something…

I know almost nothing about SEO. I told her that upfront. She looked at me like I was slightly insane for offering to help.

But here’s the thing. You don’t need to know the industry. You need to know the tool and lean on the domain experts that do.

We sat down and I started asking her questions.

- What does the spreadsheet look like?

- What are you actually looking for when you scan it manually?

- What makes a keyword negative?


She explained it. I listened. Then we built a Claude Project around exactly that logic — her criteria, her process, her standards — and walked through the workflow together.

20 minutes later she could run the whole analysis in ten minutes.

She stared at the screen for a second. Then she said:

“Wait. Can we do this for the other part too?”

**That’s the look.**

If you’ve done this with someone, you know exactly what I’m describing. It isn’t excitement. It’s reorientation.

> The moment someone’s mental model of what’s possible quietly shifts **and doesn’t shift back.**

### **The Gap Nobody Talks About**

Here’s where it gets strange.

I was at a Supabase hackathon two weeks ago. The developers in that room knew more about code than I will in my entire career. These are people who write software for a living, who have been doing it for years, who are genuinely excellent at it.

They were slow.

Not struggling. Not failing. Slow. Still working the old way. Still treating AI like a slightly faster version of Stack Overflow rather than a fundamentally different way of working.

It turns out this isn’t unusual.

just published a video where he talked about a randomized control trial published last year by METR found that experienced developers using AI tools took 19% longer to complete tasks than developers working without them. The researchers controlled for task difficulty, experience level, and tool familiarity. None of it mattered.

But what’s even weirder…those same developers believed AI had made them 24% faster.

Wrong about the direction.

Wrong about the magnitude.

Wrong in both directions at once.

And that’s the real gap right now. Not between people who use AI and people who don’t.

> **Between people who’ve rebuilt their workflow around it and people who’ve bolted it on top of the old one.**

Deep technical expertise does not automatically translate.

Years of experience do not automatically translate.

The developers at that hackathon had mastery I’ll never have. Didn’t matter. I (and other AI maniacs) knew something they didn’t.

That asymmetry is everywhere right now, across every industry, at every level of seniority.

The people who know the most about their field are often the ones most locked into how they’ve always worked.

### **The Business You’ve Never Heard Of**

On a recent episode of My First Million, Shaan Puri described visiting his friend’s father. The man runs an at-home care company in Missouri. Ninety million dollars a year in revenue. Eighty employees. Seventy-plus years old.

Puri asked him about AI.

The owner had never heard the word. Not skeptical of it. Not dismissing it. Had never encountered it as a concept. Puri walked into the office and described it as eighty employees who all looked like the same person, straight out of Ferris Bueller’s Day Off.

Eight million dollars a year in payroll. Puri’s reaction on the show:

> “That’s just one little business you’ve never heard of, in the middle of Missouri, that just unlocked $8 million a year in value that someone can go and do.”

Think about how many of those businesses exist. Not just in Missouri. In every mid-sized city, in every industry you’ve never worked in, run by people who are excellent at what they do and have never heard the word AI used in a sentence that applied to them.

This is the market.

It is enormous.

And almost nobody in this community is talking to it.

* * *

### **Why Most People Are Paralyzed**

Cedric Chin, whose response to all of it was that single tweet above, had the right instinct:

> Stop consuming emotional reactions to AI and pay attention to field reports from people actually using it.

That reframe matters more than it might seem.

Because 80 million people who read that doom article are now feeling something. Fear, mostly. Paralysis, for a lot of them. The comments are full of people asking what they should do. And the answer, _the actual answer,_ is embarrassingly simple.

**Use the tool.**

Not read about the tool.

Not worry about the tool.

Not watch videos about people using the tool.

Use it. On real work. With real people. In real situations where something is actually at stake.

The doom scroll doesn’t give you that. The viral article doesn’t give you that. Sitting in a room with someone who reviews eight thousand keywords manually and saying “let me see if I can help.”

That gives you that.

### **You Are Not Behind**

Here’s what doesn’t get said enough in rooms like this one.

You are not behind.

> You are so far ahead that the gap is genuinely hard to see from where you’re standing.

The water you’re swimming in feels normal because you’ve been in it long enough. Claude Projects, Skills, custom workflows, these are just Monday for you. But for most of the world, this isn’t water. It’s the deep end, and nobody told them to bring a swimsuit.

The SEO employee had been doing that keyword review for years. She built her entire workflow around it. She was good at it. She had processes, she had spreadsheets, she had a rhythm. She had absolutely no reason to think a stranger who admitted knowing nothing about her industry could help her.

**But knowing her industry wasn’t the point.**

Sam Parr made this observation on that same My First Million episode. The problem, he said, is that we compare to the two decimal places over. Elon’s doing this. That person raised that. This company shipped that. And we freeze, convinced we’re behind when we’re actually lapping most of the field.

> You don’t have to be them to win.

Catching any piece of this puts you ahead of almost everyone. The managing partner of a major law firm who figured this out isn’t using AI because it’s a toy. He’s using it because it works, and he told someone recently that it’s like having a team of associates available instantly. He’s not panicking. But he’s paying very close attention.

The person already experimenting, already building, already paying attention (which is you, reading this right now) is better positioned than almost anyone to take advantage of what’s sitting right in front of them.

* * *

### **What You Can Actually Do This Week**

The opportunity doesn’t require expertise in the field you’re helping.

It doesn’t require you to understand SEO, or at-home care, or whatever industry the person across from you has spent their career in. It requires you to know the tool well enough to show someone something they haven’t seen. That’s it. One proof point. One “wait, it can do that?” moment.

Here’s the concrete version of that.

Find someone this week who is drowning in a process they’ve been doing manually for years. It doesn’t have to be a big process. It doesn’t have to be complicated. It just has to be something that takes more time than it should and that they’ve accepted as just the way things are.

Sit down with them. Ask them to explain what they’re actually doing…

- The steps

- The logic

- The decisions they’re making


Listen carefully. You’re not there to impress them with AI. You’re there to understand their problem well enough to show them a better way.

Then build something simple. A Project. A prompt. A workflow that takes their criteria and does the heavy lifting. Don’t overpromise. Don’t pitch. Just show them.

30 minutes. That’s usually enough to change how someone sees what’s possible. And once someone has seen what’s possible, the conversation about what comes next takes care of itself. You don’t have to close anything. You don’t have to sell anything. The proof does that work for you.

A day’s worth of manual work down to ten minutes is its own argument.

The people consuming the doom content are waiting for someone to tell them what to do. You already know what to do.

The doom scroll can wait.
